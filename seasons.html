Spring

In springtime, herbs start growing leaves. In parks and places with soil and trees, you'll see the first bulb plants like ramson and sand leek popping up. There's also wood sorrel, which you can find all year round. In playgrounds and gardens at home, you might spot ground elder, red dead nettle, and garlic mustard. In city parks, you can pick young leaves from trees like spruce, which start growing leaves at the end of April. On grassy lawns, you'll find plants like common sorrel, daisies, and dandelions. Near the sea, you'll see special plants near harbors, and you can even collect seaweed from clean beaches.

Summer

In summer, you can find tasty flowers like elderflower and sorrel growing near bushes in parks and along paths where people ride bicycles. Roses and rosehips also bloom during this time. Horseradish starts popping up near roads and close to grassy areas where kids play. The cherries on wild cherry trees that line the roads get ripe in the middle of summer. Around the same time, chicory starts growing on the edges of towns, and plants that live in water start growing along lakes, rivers, and seas. Towards the end of summer, some herbs from spring become too hard and not very tasty, but plants like stinging nettles and other leafy greens will keep growing if they're regularly cut down.

Autumn

In late summer, you can find yummy blackberries and rowan berries ready to eat. Wild apples and pears, which are like the main fruits of autumn in towns, take until September to be just right. In that same month, mugwort starts blooming in places with dry, sandy soil, like empty lots and the sides of roads. When September and October come around, mushrooms start popping up in forests and parks where old branches and leaves are breaking down.
